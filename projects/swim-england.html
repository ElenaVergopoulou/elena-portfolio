Overview

The Swim England UX case study presents a detailed usability evaluation of Swim England’s official website. Conducted within the scope of a university module, the project aimed to assess whether users could easily navigate the site, locate essential services, and understand its broader purpose.

The research employed moderated usability testing sessions with five participants, covering key user journeys including volunteering, coaching, learning to swim, and using the PoolFinder tool. Findings were analyzed through structured qualitative methods, culminating in actionable recommendations to improve navigation clarity, label consistency, and overall user orientation.

The Swim England homepage as seen by users during the study. Key elements like the PoolFinder and site purpose are not immediately clear.

Project Goals

  1. Evaluate homepage clarity and first impressions: Understand whether the website immediately communicates its purpose and target users.
  2. Assess navigation structure and key content discovery: Explore how easily users can find crucial information such as volunteering, coaching, and events.
  3. Test the usability and discoverability of PoolFinder: Determine if users notice and understand the PoolFinder tool, and if they find it useful.
  4. Evaluate the Learn to Swim pathway experience: Observe how different users find information on learning stages and progression.

My Role

I led the end-to-end research process, including:

  • Designing tasks aligned to evaluation goals
  • Recruiting and moderating 5 usability sessions
  • Recording (OBS), transcribing (Otter.ai), and coding findings
  • Building an affinity map and rainbow spreadsheet
  • Delivering a stakeholder-ready report

Methodology

Participants

5 casual swimmers with varied digital skills, given role-based tasks (e.g., parent, teacher).

Task Design

  • 1 exploratory task (homepage)
  • 6 scenario tasks (navigation, PoolFinder, Learn to Swim, etc.)

Sample Tasks from Usability Script

Q1 – Homepage Impressions

  • Spend a few moments exploring the site. What do you think Swim England does?
  • Who do you think the website is aimed at?
  • How visually appealing do you find the homepage?

Q2 – Volunteering, Coaching, and Teaching

  • You want to volunteer at swimming events. Find out how to register your interest.
  • You want to become a swimming coach. Explore how to begin your coaching journey.
  • You’re either a Level 1 swimming teacher wanting to advance to Level 2, or a newcomer aiming for Level 1. Find the relevant training path.
  • You’re an artistic swimmer interested in competing in an April event. Locate details like date, location, and registration steps.

Q3 – PoolFinder

  • You live in Shoreditch and want to find a local swimming pool using PoolFinder.

Q4 – Learn to Swim Pathway

  • You’re an intermediate swimmer looking into diving or artistic swimming classes.
  • You’re a parent seeking beginner-level swim classes for a 4-year-old.
  • Your child just completed stage 1 or 2. Find out what comes next.

Setup

  • 4 remote, 1 in-person session (30–45 mins)
  • Think-aloud protocol, recorded with OBS

Analysis

Annotations captured usability issues, successes, and general feedback. An affinity map and rainbow spreadsheet were used.

Rainbow Spreadsheet used to track and prioritize usability issues across participants.

Findings

Navigation & Findability

  • Key information like volunteering or event registration was hard to find
  • “Useful Links” was often ignored or missed
  • Labels were misleading (“Awards” for Learn to Swim stages)
  • Some participants found links via homepage blocks or ads, leading to uncertainty about their reliability
  • The menu lacked consistency and hierarchy

Navigation: “Teachers” links appear in multiple places, confusing users.

PoolFinder Tool

  • Users praised PoolFinder once they found it, calling it “the easiest part of the site”
  • Major issue: it was hidden in the gray navigation bar and often mistaken for a static banner
  • Labels like “Regions” and “Operators” misled users
  • Some assumed clickable tags weren’t interactive or vice versa

PoolFinder: The upper navigation bar was ignored or mistaken as a banner.

PoolFinder: Some users didn’t realize tags were clickable due to unclear visual cues.

Learn to Swim Pathway

  • Users struggled to find stage-specific information
  • “Awards” was not a logical place to look for swim progression
  • Navigation structure subtly changed within the Learn to Swim section, disorienting users

Learn to Swim: Users didn’t expect progression stages to be hidden under “Awards.”

Recommendations

The final recommendations emerged directly from patterns observed across multiple users and were supported by behavioral evidence in the rainbow spreadsheet and annotated transcripts. These have been expanded with clearer design intent and practical next steps:

Clarify Website Purpose and Audience

Several users misunderstood the site’s intent, assuming it was only for beginners or parents. A clear homepage tagline such as:

“Swim England: Supporting swimmers, parents, coaches, and clubs across the UK.”

would help users orient themselves immediately and reduce cognitive load.

Streamline Navigation into a Single System

Issues stemmed from the presence of multiple menus (gray nav bar, homepage tiles, “Useful Links”). Merge into one coherent structure using dropdowns grouped by journey (e.g., “Start Swimming,” “Volunteer,” “Train to Teach”).

Re-label Ambiguous or Misleading Menu Items

Terms like “Awards” failed to convey progression stages. Replace with intuitive labels like “Swim Levels” or “Progression Stages.” Remove duplicates like multiple “Teachers” links to reduce confusion.

Signal Domain Transitions Clearly

Subtle changes in content and layout led to disorientation. Use background color shifts, breadcrumb trails, or subtitle banners to signal transitions between subdomains (e.g., “Learn to Swim”).

These enriched recommendations directly address the observed pain points and are implementation-ready. In a real-world client context, I would follow up with low-fidelity wireframes and stakeholder testing to validate these ideas.

Outcome

This project was part of a Master's-level HCI module and approved for external sharing with Swim England. It received strong feedback for:

  • Clear planning and structured execution
  • Effective use of think-aloud and scenario-based testing
  • Detailed behavioral analysis tied to project goals
  • Well-organized and visually supported reporting

The tutor highlighted the depth of usability insights and praised the report’s clarity. One area of improvement was the need for more visual or design-driven recommendations, which I’ve since addressed through this iteration.

This outcome reaffirmed my interest in UX research and strengthened my ability to connect user insights to tangible design improvements.

Reflection

This case study was a pivotal experience that challenged me to manage a full UX research pipeline. Key highlights include:

What Went Well

  • Strategic test planning: Each task was aligned with a research goal. Separating open-ended exploration from functional tasks surfaced deeper insights.
  • Qualitative rigor: Manual coding, affinity mapping, and the rainbow spreadsheet enabled me to extract themes and validate issues across users.
  • User empathy: Scenarios reflected real Swim England use cases, designed with inclusivity and clarity in mind.

Areas to Improve

  • Incorporating SUS scores or confidence ratings would enrich the qualitative data with quantitative anchors.
  • Initial recommendations lacked visual fidelity. In future iterations, I’ll supplement suggestions with mockups or annotated wireframes.

This project reinforced the importance of research that’s not only methodical but also human-centered. It taught me that effective UX isn’t just about identifying friction—it's about translating those insights into clearer, kinder, more accessible experiences.
