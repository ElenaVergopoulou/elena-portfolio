Overview

This qualitative research project explored how users plan leisure and travel activities using digital tools in both remote and in-person settings. Grounded in Human-Computer Interaction (HCI) theory, the study applied two key frameworks‚ÄîMental Models (for remote, screen-based tasks) and Distributed Cognition for Teams (DiCoT) (for in-person, situated tasks).

The goal was to uncover interaction patterns, identify usability breakdowns, and generate concrete, theory-driven design recommendations for future travel planning tools.

Project Goals

  1. Investigate how users interact with digital travel tools in naturalistic settings: Observe how tools are used both remotely and in-person for planning activities.
  2. Apply HCI theory (Mental Models and DiCoT): Use these frameworks to extract actionable insights from real-world usage patterns.
  3. Identify mismatches between user expectations and system behavior: Analyze pain points in interaction flows and tool comprehension.
  4. Explore the role of physical and social context: Examine how shared spaces, collaboration, and artefacts shape cognitive offloading and task success.
  5. Generate theory-informed design principles: Provide guidelines for improving usability in future travel-related digital systems.

My Role

This was an individual research project, and I was responsible for leading every stage of the study‚Äîfrom design to analysis. My contributions included:

  • Designing the study methodology, securing ethical approval, and aligning the research with HCI theory
  • Recruiting participants and conducting both remote (screen-based) and in-person (situated) observations
  • Using the think-aloud protocol and screen recording tools to gather detailed qualitative data
  • Manually coding the transcripts to identify recurring patterns and usability breakdowns
  • Analyzing user behavior through the lenses of Mental Models and Distributed Cognition (DiCoT)
  • Writing the full academic report with theory-informed design recommendations for future digital travel systems

Methodology

The study used a two-pronged ethnographic method to explore how users plan leisure and travel activities in both remote and in-person contexts.

Remote Observations

  • Conducted with three participants using platforms like Skyscanner, Omio, and Booking.com
  • Sessions applied the Mental Models framework to examine user expectations, mismatches, prior knowledge, and adaptive behavior
  • Participants verbalized thoughts using the think-aloud protocol while performing authentic planning tasks

In-Person Observations

  • Conducted with three additional participants during situated decision-making (e.g., navigating museums, choosing a place to eat)
  • Analysis followed the Distributed Cognition for Teams (DiCoT) framework, focusing on spatial layout, information flow, artefacts, and environmental constraints

Analysis & Coding

  • All sessions were recorded and transcribed
  • Manual thematic coding was performed using categories drawn directly from HCI theory
  • Findings were mapped to design implications and structured into theory-informed recommendations

This mixed-context methodology allowed for a rich understanding of both cognitive and environmental factors influencing digital travel behavior.

Findings

Analysis was divided between two frameworks‚ÄîMental Models (for remote screen-based use) and Distributed Cognition for Teams (DiCoT, for in-person tasks). Both highlighted recurring breakdowns in user expectations and revealed how users adapt to technological and environmental constraints.

Mental Models (Remote Observations)

  • Expectations: Users anticipated familiar features like sort-by-price, filters by time or transport type, and consistent search behavior.
  • Mismatches: Unexpected results (e.g., fluctuating prices, missing filters, misleading map previews) created friction and confusion.
  • Adaptations: Users often shifted their strategy‚Äîswitching between transport modes, relaxing filters, or abandoning tasks mid-way.
  • Prior Knowledge: Familiarity with tools led to workarounds, like manually cross-checking prices on multiple platforms.

‚Üí Users followed the pattern: Expectation ‚Üí Mismatch ‚Üí Adaptation, suggesting that travel tools frequently violate users‚Äô mental models and require cognitive compensation.

DiCoT Framework (In-Person Observations)

  • Physical Layout: Decisions were highly influenced by immediate proximity (e.g., choosing the closest food option).
  • Artefacts: Visual tools like maps, star ratings, and user images became key decision triggers.
  • Information Flow: Users rapidly toggled across apps (e.g., Google Maps ‚Üí TripAdvisor ‚Üí Instagram) in real-time decision loops.
  • Social/Temporal Factors: Group consensus, time pressure, and social heuristics (e.g., ‚Äúmost reviewed‚Äù = ‚Äúmost trustworthy‚Äù) shaped actions.

‚Üí Decision-making was distributed across devices, tools, environments, and time‚Äîconfirming the applicability of the DiCoT model even in solo, real-world scenarios.

Recommendations

Based on the dual-framework analysis, the following design recommendations aim to improve usability, reduce cognitive friction, and align with real-world planning behaviors observed in both remote and in-person settings.

Recommendations from Mental Models (Remote Observations)

  • Align with User Expectations: Implement familiar filter and sort features (e.g., by price, duration, or rating) and ensure consistency across pages to reduce surprises.
  • Clarify System States: Clearly indicate when prices change, options are sold out, or filters reset‚Äîthis builds user trust and prevents confusion.
  • Support Flexible Adjustments: Enable users to switch between transport modes or trip criteria without having to restart the entire task.

Recommendations from DiCoT (In-Person Observations)

  • Enhance Spatial Awareness: Embed walking time or real-time travel estimates directly in listings to support situated decision-making.
  • Unify Information Sources: Integrate ratings and review summaries from third-party apps (like Google Maps or TripAdvisor) into the core interface to minimize app-switching.
  • Provide Time-Sensitive Feedback: Include dynamic alerts or visual cues when places may soon close or become unavailable, improving trust and reducing last-minute stress.

These recommendations can inform the design of future travel planning tools‚Äîespecially those used in hybrid digital-physical settings‚Äîand are grounded in both cognitive and contextual insights from real users.

Outcome

This research project culminated in a comprehensive academic report that combined observational data, cognitive theory, and design practice to address real-world usability issues in digital travel planning.

  • Grounded insights into how users navigate planning tasks in both digital and physical environments
  • Rich qualitative data‚Äîincluding transcripts and think-aloud sessions‚Äîcoded through Mental Models and DiCoT frameworks
  • Actionable, theory-informed UX design guidelines for improving remote and situated interactions
  • A clear demonstration of how HCI theory can support both academic understanding and practical interface design

The study validated the value of combining Mental Models and DiCoT to capture a holistic view of user experience‚Äîbridging internal expectations with contextual, embodied behavior. These outcomes offer transferable insights for designing hybrid travel tools that support planning, decision-making, and real-time adaptation.
