Overview

The project aimed to enhance the visitor experience at the Imperial War Museum through the design of an interactive kiosk focused on the V2 rocket exhibit. The final outcome was a Rocket Constructor and Simulator‚Äîa sensory, educational, and interactive experience enabling users to explore the history and evolution of rocket technology in a hands-on format.

The kiosk experience was shaped by mixed-methods user research, user-centered design principles, and iterative prototyping. It combined passive and active learning modes to suit diverse visitor preferences, fostering emotional and educational engagement through storytelling and environmental simulation.

Final project poster summarizing the Rocket Constructor and Simulator concept.

Project Goals

  1. Enhance engagement in a historically significant but emotionally challenging exhibit: Use interactivity to create meaningful and age-appropriate visitor experiences around the V2 rocket.
  2. Encourage learning through active participation and storytelling: Support visitor understanding by combining simulation, exploration, and narrative elements.
  3. Balance sensory and informational design: Offer both playful interaction and contemplative reflection to suit diverse visitor preferences.
  4. Respect ethical boundaries: Shift the focus from the V2 rocket‚Äôs destruction toward its technological evolution, avoiding glorification.
  5. Align with museum constraints: Design within physical, accessibility, and curatorial limitations of the Imperial War Museum.

Findings

Immersive Experience

  • Visitors strongly favored sensory features‚Äîciting earthquake or submarine simulators in other museums as benchmarks.
  • Sound, vibration, and airflow were described as highly engaging, contributing to emotional immersion.

Learning & Information Preferences

  • Learning was a core driver for both children and adult visitors, especially in family groups.
  • Large blocks of static text were perceived as overwhelming and off-putting.
  • Users preferred structured content delivered through visual storytelling or guided progression.

Interaction & Navigation

  • The hands-on rocket construction phase was appreciated, but rigid drag-and-drop elements reduced enjoyment.
  • Visitors valued having control over the experience‚Äîbeing able to choose between passive (watching) and active (building) paths.
  • Story-driven navigation was praised for making the content more memorable and accessible.

Recommendations

The following recommendations emerged from observational research, interviews, and usability testing. They are grounded in user feedback and aligned with inclusive, engaging design principles tailored for a museum setting:

Increase Flexibility in Drag-and-Drop Interaction

Visitors found the Rocket Constructor engaging but occasionally restrictive. Introducing more freedom in element placement and clearer feedback cues would improve enjoyment and encourage creative exploration.

Reduce Cognitive Load with Visual Aids

Replace dense text blocks with visual storytelling elements‚Äîicons, images, and progressive disclosure. Chunking information and layering content would help users absorb key messages without feeling overwhelmed.

Improve Flow and Stage Transitions

Users occasionally lost context between construction, simulation, and reflection stages. Adding subtle transition animations or audio prompts would help reinforce narrative continuity and reduce friction.

Enhance Visual and Sensory Design

Future iterations should include richer illustrations, subtle animations, and tactile feedback elements (e.g., sound, vibration) to elevate emotional engagement and replicate the positive reactions to sensory features in other exhibits.

Expand Post-Visit Engagement Options

Allow users to print or email their custom rocket designs and offer donation prompts tied to educational support. These actions would strengthen memory retention and visitor loyalty beyond the museum visit.

These recommendations were designed with scalability in mind and could be iterated through stakeholder feedback, higher-fidelity prototyping, and continued user testing in a live exhibit environment.

Outcome

The final deliverable was a multi-stage interactive prototype designed for use in a museum kiosk. The Rocket Constructor and Simulator experience included the following elements:

  • Rocket Selection & Info Screen: Users explored 3D models of A/V-series rockets and accessed contextual historical content.
  • Rocket Construction: A drag-and-drop interface allowed users to build rockets while engaging with pop-up educational facts.
  • Sensory Launch Simulation: A multi-sensory experience including sound, vibration, and airflow effects to simulate a rocket launch.
  • Print & Donate: Users could 3D print their custom rocket or donate to support museum education initiatives.

The prototype was well received by tutors and peers for its originality, emotional depth, and alignment with museum constraints. It successfully met the project brief by delivering a hands-on, story-driven learning experience grounded in user research and inclusive design principles.

Reflection

This project deepened my understanding of how to balance emotional sensitivity with interactive design in museum contexts. Designing around a weapon of war‚Äîwhile honoring both history and human impact‚Äîrequired careful consideration in both tone and content.

What Went Well

  • Applying ethical UX principles: We shifted the narrative focus from destruction to technological evolution, encouraging respectful curiosity over spectacle.
  • Interdisciplinary collaboration: Working with teammates from diverse backgrounds enhanced the experience design with varied pedagogical and emotional perspectives.
  • Visual storytelling: I led the creation of posters and storyboards that guided the user's emotional and educational journey through the kiosk.

Areas to Improve

  • While the low-fidelity prototype validated the concept and flow, a higher-fidelity version would better communicate visual design intentions and interactive depth.
  • Expanding accessibility testing‚Äîespecially for users with sensory sensitivities‚Äîwould strengthen inclusivity in future iterations.
  • Adding personalization features such as a ‚Äúmemory booklet‚Äù or post-visit souvenir could enhance long-term impact and engagement.

Overall, this project showed me the power of storytelling as a tool for education and empathy. It pushed me to think critically about how interactive media can inform, engage, and move people‚Äîespecially in spaces where history and emotion intersect.
